Project Description

As technology continues to advance, the use of social media has become a popular form of communication and interaction among people of various ages worldwide. Many studies have explored the impact that social media usage has on younger populations; however, fewer studies have considered adult populations. Accordingly, we wish to further explore whether the frequency and duration of social media use impact the mental health and well being of adult populations. The research questions we will explore are as follows: Does being a high/low-frequency social media user affect the willingness to believe information about how social media usage impacts one’s mental health and well-being? And, does being a high/low-frequency social media user influence amenability to change usage patterns when presented with data regarding the correlation between high usage and poor outcomes?”

Hypotheses

  1. Increased social media use among younger adults will have a more negative impact on their mental health, while older adults will report fewer adverse effects on their mental well-being.

  2. Higher frequency social media users exposed to information regarding the negative impact of higher social media usage on mental health will report less likelihood to change their usage patterns, while lower frequency users will be more amenable to reduced their time on social media.

Descriptive Summary

The dataset consists of 263 responses to the survey.

Six questions about social media use and mental health were asked prior to the respondent receiving the treatment - one of three infographics presenting neutral, positive, and negative impacts of social media use.

Following the treatment respondents were asked four additional questions:

  • to rank how frequently their social media use made them feel 10 different emotions (five negative, five positive) on a five-option Likert scale (never, sometimes, about half the time, most of the time, always).
  • whether they had made friends because of their social media use
  • whether social media made them feel more/less connected to others
  • whether they had ever tried to reduce their social media use

Finally, respondents answered four demographic questions related to age, gender, ethnicity, and education level.

Respondents who use social media up to one hour and 1-2 hours per day were most likely to report good mental health (41% and 39% respectively). We can use a chi-square test to determine whether there is a
